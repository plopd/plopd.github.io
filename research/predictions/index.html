Notes

The motivation given in the paper 1 for representing machine knowledge as predictions is to give the agent the ability to construct its own world knowledge of the world, and not to depend on an external designer.

If we consider that the only thing an agent can control is its own stream of experience coming in from the world, then knowledge can be constructed from sensations about that stream, the agent’s past and subsequent behavior of having taken an action, and time.

General Value Functions (GVFs) extend value functions by generalizing a single scalar reward signal to any signal, called a cumulant. Note that this does not interfere with the main goal of reinforcement learning, which is to maximize the cumulative sum of rewards. These cumulants are linked to the reward that serves their maximization.

A prediction can be defined as a linear combination of the weights that parameterize the GVF and the agent state features of an observation. The question parameters are the weights of the GVF, the discount function, an action taken from a policy, and the policy itself describing the behavior followed. The answer parameters, such as the step size and the decay rate of the fitness trace, specify how the prediction is learned through the optimization process.

The take-home message of the paper 1 is that a prediction can be accurate (e.g., in response error) but not useful. In the experiment, two different predictions are learned. Both are specified by the same GVF, and the only difference between them is the feature approximation used to construct the agent-state feature. Given a signal (or stimulus) of interest, one prediction learns to track the signal on a moment-by-moment basis, while the other learns to anticipate the change in the stimulus, increasing just before the stimulus is about to rise, and conversely decreasing just before the stimulus is about to fall.

The first prediction is accurate, in terms of return error, but not as useful as the second prediction, which learns to predict the signal of interest. Most would agree that the second prediction is more useful to an agent that wants to control its environment through adaptive behavior.

So, with so many predictions that an agent can make to build world knowledge, it is important to evaluate the usefulness of the prediction not only in terms of its accuracy, but also in terms of its usefulness for taking better actions.
